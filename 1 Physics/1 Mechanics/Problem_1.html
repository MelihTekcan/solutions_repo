Investigating the Range as a Function of the Angle of Projection

1. Theoretical Foundation

Projectile motion is governed by Newton’s laws under constant gravitational acceleration. The key equations are derived as follows:

  • Horizontal Motion: No acceleration, so \( x(t) = v_0 \cos\theta \cdot t \).
  • Vertical Motion: Acceleration \( a_y = -g \), yielding \( y(t) = h + v_0 \sin\theta \cdot t - \frac{1}{2} g t^2 \).
  • Time of Flight: Solve \( y(t) = 0 \):
    • For \( h = 0 \): \( t = \frac{2 v_0 \sin\theta}{g} \).
    • For \( h > 0 \): \( t = \frac{v_0 \sin\theta + \sqrt{(v_0 \sin\theta)^2 + 2 g h}}{g} \).
  • Range:
    • For \( h = 0 \): \( R = \frac{v_0^2 \sin 2\theta}{g} \).
    • For \( h > 0 \): \( R = v_0 \cos\theta \cdot t \) with the above \( t \).

This forms a family of solutions parameterized by initial velocity (\( v_0 \)), angle (\( \theta \)), gravity (\( g \)), and height (\( h \)).

Key Observations:

  • Angle Dependence: Maximum range occurs at 45° when \( h = 0 \), where \( \sin 2\theta = 1 \).
  • Velocity Effect: \( R \propto v_0^2 \), so doubling \( v_0 \) quadruples the range.
  • Gravity Effect: \( R \propto \frac{1}{g} \), e.g., range on the Moon (\( g = 1.62 \, \text{m/s}^2 \)) is ~6 times that on Earth.
  • Height Effect: Non-zero \( h \) increases flight time, shifting the optimal angle slightly below 45°.

  • Astrophysics: On Mars (\( g = 3.72 \, \text{m/s}^2 \)), ranges are 2.6 times longer than on Earth.

4. Limitations and Extensions

This idealized model assumes:

  • No air resistance: Real projectiles experience drag (\( F_d = -k v^2 \)), requiring numerical solutions.
  • Flat terrain: Uneven surfaces need adjusted landing conditions.
  • No wind: Lateral forces would alter \( v_{0x} \).

Extensions:

  • Use numerical methods (e.g., Runge-Kutta) to include drag.
  • Simulate variable \( g \) for high-altitude trajectories.
